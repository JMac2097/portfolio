Eca Final Report
By John McKie
Effective communication


After hours of decision-making and trying to think of a good idea for my final ECA project, I settled on making up a website for a fictional night club called Unified.
Then I had to think about what the site would be used for.  I managed to do a fair bit of research into this, I collected samples from quite a few real nightclub websites,  most were ghastly, and from this collection I concluded what the average user would expect to find on a nightclub homepage.
I  identified that the main purpose of the site would be to advertise, not only the club, but also the events that would be happening at the club.  So it would be a balance between commercial (making money from ticket sales) and Informative (keeping the user up to date with upcoming events). I decided that the club would also include live bands to make it more varied and to allow me to put more information into my site.  The main user group that I was aiming it at were the 21 year olds to 31 year olds age group (older people are welcome too) and I wanted to get away from that whole "larging it" feel that most nightclub sites seem to promote and go with something a little fresh and different.
I set out that these users would be looking for an exciting venue to spend an evening in, they would also be looking for variety, so I tried to represent many different musical genres and tastes on the site by having many different genre-based club nights. So, in the end, I decided that the keyword for the construction of my site would be "exciting".
To achieve this feel of excitement, I included a few large images of  dancers, djs playing music, and a live band, these were all quite exciting looking "action" images which I managed to get hold of thanks to Flickr (http://www.flickr.com accessed 17 June 2008) and I displayed one main image per page to keep the site neat-looking and to tie-in with the information that was displayed on the relative pages.
While designing my site, I came up with a few ideas to add extra levels of interaction.  Having not long before read about interaction in Lesson 9, the idea appealed to me.
The first would be an online order form which I created a link to on the bookings page, but of course, the form doesn't actually exist.  I put a notice on the site to say that the link is currently out of action, but this form would be along similar lines as the type you can find on on-line shopping sites, where you have to enter card details and quantities of tickets etc..
Another feature that I would include would be a music sampler.  This would allow the user to click on the band that they were interested in seeing live and they would not only be presented with a form containing more details about the band, there would also be an online music player to allow the user to sample snippets of tracks from the band, or even a media player that would play video clips. Obviously coding something like that would be way above my level at this time but it would simply involve the use of an appropriate plug-in, eg Shockwave.
The final idea that I would incorporate would be a bulletin board where the user could post comments about the events they were excited about seeing or the events that they'd enjoyed being at, or even post up a review of one of the bands.  This feature would be very useful and get the users talking amongst themselves, and they could even arrange to meet up.  Taking the interactive element even further.


Site design, ease of navigation and accessibility


I followed the guidelines for the structure of my site that I was shown in Lesson 7 session 2.  Although the project site is relatively small, I decided that the best option would be to make the site web-like, so every page could be reached from every other page, laying out the design of the site in a web-like structure.  If the site was any larger, then I could have considered using a hierarchical structure, starting with the index page and moving down from there.
Simplicity was a word that I kept in my mind, I wanted this site to be both visually appealing, but at the same time incredibly simple and usable, the user should just log in, find what they want, and go from there, which is where accessibility becomes a factor.  I had to design a site that allows the user to adjust the text size and all images have to contain alternative text in case a user is utilising a device, such as a screen reader.
Working on a separate piece of paper, I set out what information each of the pages would contain.  I managed to break it down into five groups for each of the five pages that were required in the design brief, also including the Index and report page.
The page titles I settled on were: Gig guide, Club nights, Bookings, Location and finally, About us.  The Index page contains a brief run down of what the site contains, including hyperlinks to the other pages as well as the primary navigation.  The Gig guide, I decided, would primarily be a table containing information about live bands that were playing.  The Club nights page would be similar to the previous Gig guide, because the information it needs to display is similar too.  The Bookings page contains information about ticket booking, but as I've already said in the first section, the link to the online form doesn't work, so I decided to put up  a warning on screen to say the the link was currently under maintenance.  The Location page would contain a map of the area and the club's made up address (that information is also displayed in the main header on each page).  Finally, the About us page, put in for the user to contact the club.  This page could also be the home of the bulletin board that I mentioned previously.
Navigation was a main concern for me, having again read through Lesson 7  session 3, I had to ensure that my navigation answers the key user questions:
Where am I? Where have I been? Where can I go next? How do I return to where I started?
I decided to make my navigation a simple horizontal bar across the top of  the page under the main header, putting it in the centre and at the top which is where the user looks for important information, according to Joel Sklar's screen map, which was mentioned in Lesson 2. Each page is also clearly headed and highlighted when displayed on screen in the navigation bar.  The user can also travel anywhere they like using this navigation, obviously it may need to be restricted once the site gets bigger to stop users getting lost.
This literally being a one man operation, the only testing that I could invoke was to upload my pages using Firefox and view them on-screen to see how the design was progressing.  This however does have disadvantages.  The page can sometimes look radically different in the NVU work window than it does in the actual browser window.
Not having a client or an actual user base to feed back ideas and concepts to is also restrictive, because even though I feel that I have met the design brief quite well,  I know this having opened up my final site and taken a User trip around it, who's to say that the client I would be designing for would agree with me, or even the users?  So in a real design environment, I would be feeding ideas to the client, and when we had found an idea that worked, then some proper user testing could be carried out.
  
 
Page design, layout, use of fonts, use of images, use of colour


When I finally got down to the serious work of putting the design on screen, I loaded up NVU, and had a bit of a play around with tables and such, realising that the use of tables pretty much allowed me to place whatever I liked wherever I liked on the page.  This brief period of experimenting was quite valuable, because it got me back into the feel of NVU and all its little quirks (freezing when you have lots of work on the screen for one).  
After experimentation, I then proceeded to start mocking up pencil versions of what the main index page would look like, I came up with six rough layouts which I had designed keeping in mind the design information that I learned way back in lesson 2, which was Robin Williams' acronym PARC: Proximity, Alignment, Repetition and Contrast. I also tried to keep in mind Joel Sklar's screen map, which detailed the levels of importance of elements on the screen, which I also learned about in lesson 2.  Keeping these two factors in mind, I drew up a rough design that complied with both. All relevant areas were grouped together, all the important information was in the centre and at the top of the page, navigation and header, and I decided to make the page design in such a way so that it could be repeated throughout the website.
Working on a very rough looking, monochromatic page isn't very appealing, the urge to add colour was overwhelming.  I  resisted, as my low score on the colour deficiency test from lesson 4 revealed, colour isn't my strong point, but I can use this information to my advantage when designing a webpage so other users like myself aren't left out.  But before that, I had to get the layout perfect on NVU, and I also wanted each page to flow into the next, so that the design theme followed through and conformed to the Repetition rule from the PARC acronym.  When going from page to page, I have set the site out so that the only elements that change are the text blocks and some of the content, all of the headings and navigation remain in the same areas, at the same sizes.  All of the tables on the site are measured by screen percentage rather than being locked down to pixel size, this allows the site to be view in pretty much any size of browser screen and allowing for more acccessability.
When deciding on an appropriate font, I wanted something that would look neat and tidy at all font sizes, so it had to be a sans-serif font, as serifs do look a bit rough on a small sized font, I settled on verdana as it is a very pleasing to look at and I am comfortable with using it.  For the sake of accessibility, all font sizes are adjustable, but the default for page text is small, as verdana tends to look better the smaller it gets.
Images had to also be incorporated into the final design, I managed to get hold of quite a few relevant images by doing a search on Flickr (http://www.flickr.com accessed 17 June 2008).  I decided to use small jpg files, because of the limit of 50kb per image and 100kb limit for a page.  The images that I chose were then loaded into Irfanview (http://www.irfanview.com) and then re-saved as much smaller sized files, the image quality hasn't decreased noticeably either.  As some of the images that I had originally downloaded were grey-scaled, I decided that it would be interesting to see what would happen if all the sites images follow suit.  This worked very well as it adds contrast to the image and the coloured surroundings.
I had tried out many colour schemes, but the one which worked the best was based around different saturations of blue, a deep blue for the background and a lighter, less saturated blue for the main content boxes, The club logo uses the font Engravers MT and is a saturated yellow in direct contrast with the blue that surrounds it.  This effect draws the eye up to the company logo and contact details.  
So, in conclusion, I am quite pleased with my final site, and I hope that I have met the design brief to a high standard.  I enjoy web design greatly, and I have learned a lot from this course, and I hope to study further into this design field.
